Police Siren

Farnell synthesis study · Web Audio implementation · Comp Sound HW3

For Part II, I recreated a police siren using a logarithmic sweep oscillator, heavy horn-style distortion, a resonant band-pass filter, and short recirculating echoes to suggest reflections from nearby buildings.

Sound Goal

What I was trying to recreate

The target was the classic two-mode emergency siren: a slower wail and a faster yelp. Instead of starting from a sampled recording, I built the sound from components described in the book so the timbre would come from the oscillator shape, horn resonance, and environment rather than from playback.

Core Idea

One low-frequency oscillator sweeps a second oscillator across an 800 Hz range above 300 Hz. That raw siren is then clipped, filtered near 1.5 kHz, and sent through three short feedback delays to create the sharp, urban echo that makes it feel more realistic.

How It Works

Signal stages

1. Log oscillator

I sampled a piecewise exponential cycle that mimics capacitor charge and discharge. That shape is used for both the sweep LFO and the audible siren oscillator.

2. Frequency sweep

The sweep LFO is scaled to move the siren between about 300 Hz and 1100 Hz. The page switches between the book’s 0.1 Hz wail and 3 Hz yelp settings.

3. Horn stage

A driven gain stage and hard clipper emulate the nonlinear diaphragm. A resonant band-pass filter around 1.5 kHz shapes the added harmonics into a more horn-like tone.

4. Environmental echo

Three short delays feed a shared feedback path, which adds a compact reverberant halo like reflections from buildings around the street.

Signal Flow

Patch diagram

The most important design choice was keeping the oscillator sweep, horn shaping, and echo as distinct stages. That made it much easier to tune each part without losing the overall siren behavior.

Process

Why I made these choices

The book notes that a triangle wave is spectrally close to the more complicated logarithmic oscillator. I still chose the logarithmic version here because it matches the writeup’s method more directly and makes the implementation feel tied to the underlying charge/discharge model. In the browser patch, the horn stage matters just as much as the oscillator. Without distortion and the narrow filter, the sweep sounds too soft and too pure.

The echo section also changed the effect more than I expected. The dry siren reads as a synthetic test tone, but once the short delays are fed back together it starts to feel placed in an outdoor space.

Modes

  • Wail: 0.1 Hz sweep for the slower classic rise and fall.
  • Yelp: 3 Hz sweep for the faster urgent mode.
  • Horn filter: band-pass centered near 1.5 kHz.
  • Echo: 3 fixed delays with light shared feedback.
